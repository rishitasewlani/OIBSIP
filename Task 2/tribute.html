A tribute to the Queen of Jhansi

RANI LAKSHMI BAI

"Till my last breath, I would fight with my enemies for my mother land"

November 19, 1835 - June 17, 1858

Who was Lakshmi Bai?

Lakshmi Bai, sometimes spelt Laxmi Bai, was the ruler of Jhansi and an important participant in the Indian Mutiny of 1857â€“1858. She was born on November 19, 1835, in Kashi, India, and died at Kotah-ki-Serai, a town adjacent to Gwalior, on June 17, 1858. She was nurtured in the peshwa Baji Rao II's family, which gave her an uncommon upbringing as a Brahman girl. She was brought up in the peshwa's court, had martial arts training, and also possessed exceptional riding and sword fighting abilities.

The Bravery of the Queen of Jhansi

Jhansi ki Rani married Gangadhar Rao, the maharaja of Jhansi, but she became widowed without bearing a heir to the kingdom. Just before he passed away, the maharaja adopted a boy as his heir in accordance with accepted Hindu tradition. The adoptive successor was not acknowledged by Lord Dalhousie, the British governor-general of India, who instead conquered Jhansi in accordance with the doctrine of lapse. To handle administrative matters, an East India Company representative was assigned to the little kingdom. The queen, who was 22 years old, resisted giving the Jhansi to the British. Lakshmi Bai was named the regent of Jhansi and ruled on behalf of the minor heir shortly after the 1857 revolt that started in Meerut got under way. She quickly organised her forces, joined the revolt against the British, and took command of the insurgents in the Bundelkhand region. Mutinies in the surrounding areas moved towards Jhansi to support her. By January 1858, the East India Company's forces had started their counteroffensive in Bundelkhand under the command of Gen. Hugh Rose. Rose advanced from Mhow, took Saugor (Sagar) in February, and turned her attention to Jhansi in March. A ferocious struggle broke out when the company's men encircled the fort of Jhansi. Even after her troops were overrun and the rescuing army of Tantia Tope, another rebel leader, was routed in the Battle of Betwa, Lakshmi Bai continued to fiercely resist the advancing forces. With the help of a small group of royal guards, Lakshmi Bai was able to flee the fort and travel to the east, where she was joined by other rebels. The city-fortress of Gwalior was then successfully attacked by Tantia Tope and Lakshmi Bai. A well-known man named Nana Sahib was declared the peshwa, the treasury and the armoury being taken. After capturing Gwalior, Lakshmi Bai marched to Morar in order to meet Rose's British counterattack. She engaged in a bloody fight while dressed as a guy and lost the war.
